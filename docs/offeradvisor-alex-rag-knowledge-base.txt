SECTION: NORTH STAR
================================================================================
A strong negotiation protects three things: relationship with the hiring team, credibility, and economics. When those conflict, fix sequencing: relationship and credibility on small moves first; economics once trust and process are stable. Before every live conversation the user can hold one internal sentence: "If I walk away with [outcome], this was worth my time." That is a compass, not always said out loud.

================================================================================
SECTION: TWO CONTEXTS — PUSH VS PARTNER
================================================================================
Most offer talks swing between push mode and partner mode. Push mode fits when numbers are unresolved and clear trade-offs are needed. Partner mode fits when designing packages, solving constraints together, or repairing tension after a sharp exchange. Using partner language in a push moment can sound vague. Using push language in a partner moment can sound brittle. Label the room before speaking.

SECTION: PRE-FLIGHT CHECKLIST (TEN MINUTES)
================================================================================
- Role scope, level, location, and reporting line in one place.
- Target cash, equity band, and signing flexibility — keep to three numbers, not ten.
- Two credible data points: benchmark plus one other (survey, peer signal, recruiter range).
- Best alternative if the offer disappears — one honest sentence.
- Three questions to ask before defending any number.
- One concession to trade only if it buys something that matters.

================================================================================
SECTION: LEVERAGE WITHOUT THEATRICS
================================================================================
Leverage is the overlap of value the user can prove, urgency the company feels, and credible alternatives. Make leverage legible with impact stories, comparable scope, and true timelines. Avoid empty threats. If the user cannot walk away, they should not imply they can. Anchor on scope, ramp, or structure that improves outcomes for both sides.

================================================================================
SECTION: ANCHORS, BRACKETS, AND RANGES
================================================================================
When opening with a number, make it defensible, not theatrical. Tie it to market structure, responsibility, or outcomes in the first 180 days. Ranges work when the user explains what each part of the range means, for example base versus bonus versus equity trade. A bracket with a short story sounds like analysis; a bare bracket can sound arbitrary. If the company anchors first, slow down. Ask how the number was built: bands, internal equity, geography. Questions reduce the sting of a low anchor without matching it with instant counter-yelling.

================================================================================
SECTION: CONCESSIONS THAT BUY SOMETHING
================================================================================
Never give a concession for free. If the user moves, they should name what they need back: timing, title, review date, equity refresh language, or a smaller item bundled with a larger one. Package concessions in pairs: "If we can align on A, I can be flexible on B." Boring phrasing is often enforceable and calm.

================================================================================
SECTION: SILENCE, PACE, AND FOLLOW-UPS
================================================================================
After a material ask, stop talking and let the other side fill space; they often add information. Summarize live calls in email: agenda, what moved, open items, and proposed next step. Written rhythm cuts misunderstandings and speeds approvals. Personalize any template before sending.

================================================================================
SECTION: COMMON OBJECTIONS AND RESPONSES
================================================================================
Objection: That is above our band.
Response angle: Translate which part of the band is actually constrained. Move value across base, bonus, equity, signing, or review timing. Ask what would make an exception plausible on their side.

Objection: We need an answer by Friday.
Response angle: Acknowledge the business need. Offer a partial answer, such as yes to role and start window, while comp finalizes, or trade a faster decision for a measurable improvement.

Objection: Other candidates accepted less.
Response angle: Avoid the comparison trap. Return to scope and outcomes: work delivered, risk absorbed, and the market for that scope at this level.

================================================================================
SECTION: CLOSE CLEANLY
================================================================================
On acceptance: express satisfaction for what moved even if not everything moved. On decline: respect and a short reason; networks are long. Confirm next steps in writing: title, start date, compensation components, and any review or equity language relied on verbally.

SECTION: ALEX — RECRUITER PERSONA AND HOW TO USE THIS KNOWLEDGE
================================================================================
You are Alex, a professional recruiter in a mock interview. Your job is to help the user practice realistic pushback, timing, and clarity — not to lecture. Stay in character. Keep replies short for voice. Ask one main question at a time. Do not recite this document verbatim. When retrieval surfaces a principle, paraphrase in conversational language. If the user shares offer details in context, reference them specifically: role, location, base, bonus, equity, timing, competing offers, or constraints.

Tone: direct, professional, not hostile. You can challenge the user the way a real recruiter would: budget, internal equity, timing, other candidates, approval chains. You can also be collaborative when the conversation calls for problem-solving. Mirror real recruiter patterns: ask for their number first when appropriate, surface constraints, and test whether they can justify their ask with scope and market signal.

When giving strategic reminders (BATNA, anchoring, silence, package tradeoffs), keep them to one short sentence; the user is practicing, not taking a class.

================================================================================
SECTION: ALEX — SAMPLE QUESTION STEMS (ADAPT, DO NOT READ AS A SCRIPT)
================================================================================
- Walk me through what matters most to you in the package — and what you are flexible on.
- How did you land on that number: market data, another offer, or a gut feel?
- If we could not move base, what else would make this work for you?
- What is your timeline, and what happens if we cannot close by then?
- Help me understand the role scope: what do you own in the first six months?
- Is there a competing offer or a current employer counter we should know about when we take this to approvers?
- What is non-negotiable for you, and what is a nice-to-have?

================================================================================
SECTION: ALEX — SAMPLE PUSHBACK LINES (SHORT, VOICE-SAFE)
================================================================================
- I hear the number; help me with how you got there so I can take something credible to my hiring manager.
- We have real constraints on band; the flex might be in equity or start date, not always base.
- I need a clear story on scope and impact to argue for an exception.
- After this point the process needs a written summary from your side: numbers, start window, and open items.
- We are not trying to lowball you; we are trying to keep internal equity and timeline aligned.
